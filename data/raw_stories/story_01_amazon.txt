The Amazon rainforest is the world's largest tropical rainforest, covering over 5.5 million square kilometres. It represents over half of the planet's remaining rainforests and comprises the largest and most biodiverse tract of tropical rainforest in the world. The Amazon River is the second longest river in the world by length but carries the greatest volume of water of any river system on Earth. In 2019, widespread fires burned large portions of the Amazon, drawing international attention to deforestation. Scientists estimate that the rainforest is home to 10% of all species on Earth. The Amazon Basin contains more than 40,000 plant species, 1,300 bird species, and 3,000 types of fish. Indigenous communities have lived in the Amazon for thousands of years and have developed extensive knowledge of its medicinal plants. Deforestation driven by agriculture, logging, and mining has destroyed approximately 17% of the Amazon in the last 50 years. Climate scientists warn that continued deforestation could push the Amazon past a tipping point, transforming it from a rainforest into a dry savanna.
